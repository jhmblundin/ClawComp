**🦞OpenClaw Builder Challenge (ClawComp)**  
University students compete to build the most revolutionary OpenClaw setups possible…

## **Concept:**

ClawComp is an ongoing competition hosted by Link Ventures, where talented university teams build and present groundbreaking OpenClaw automations to win substantial prizes and networking opportunities. Link Ventures funds the computers \- students build **24/7** \- the teams accelerate each other through collaboration, and Link Ventures pioneers the most efficient and **secure** OpenClaw setup in the world. Blitzy has delivered comprehensive safety documentation on OpenClaw. While the open source community struggles to trust and merge these changes, we can take advantage of the documentation to inform the teams on how to build safely and ethically. ClawComp will have a strong social media backing and aim for **virality**. Teams will be required to document their progress and experience in the program in vlog style content. This content will filter back to Link Venture’s editing team and be posted on their social media accounts to promote the competition. A potential partnership with OpenClaw enthusiast and YouTuber [**Alex Finn**](https://www.youtube.com/@AlexFinnOfficial) would drive lots of viewership. We hope to host Alex Finn at One Kendall Square in mid June for the final ClawComp summit (ClawWeek). This summit would be hosted in Link Venture’s first floor office space and be run similarly to last year’s [**Yardhouse**](https://www.linkedin.com/company/thelaunchyard/posts/?feedView=all) hackathon. Yash, Luke, and Jack would collaborate with Alex on a video where we interview participants in ClawComp, and they walk us through their OpenClaw setups and discoveries. Overall media coverage during this week would be extensive as teams push to the finish line in hackathon spirit. ClawComp integrates easily into what student-builders are already working on. They can take whatever app, product, or research they are already conducting and integrate OpenClaw into their workflow. This solves one of the biggest pain points of traditional hackathons \- having a messy mix of pre-formed projects and truly new projects. The natural integration of ClawComp into student’s schedules allows them to be in it throughout many months of college. This gives them more time to build ties within the Link ecosystem.

**Call to Action:**

**OpenClaw** is a revolutionary LLM orchestration technology. Few student builders have the hands-on experience to realize its power. The biggest blocker here is the commitment of buying a new computer to maintain basic security. Most online content directs users to believe they need a MAC mini to run OpenClaw, which ranges from $499 \- $4599.[^1] This is the first time in the Singularity that there has been an exponential technological leap that requires builders to commit to hundreds or thousands of upfront investment \- frontier LLMs have historically been affordable to experiment with via API. OpenClaw is the new frontier of AI \- Improvements in the underlying LLMs are multiplied and compounded throughout the orchestration layers. Therefore, we believe OpenClaw is 100x as important to experiment with as pure LLMs are, and yet it is 100x less accessible to college students. There are infinitely many OpenClaw customizations and configurations yet to be tested. We believe it is important for Link Studios to become the first incubator to remove this speedbump in the Singularity. We propose ClawComp as the program to achieve this goal.

**Security Review and Considerations:**  
OpenClaw has become the fastest-growing open-source project in GitHub history, surpassing 100,000 stars within weeks of launch. However, its rapid adoption has far outpaced its security maturity, creating a risk profile that any program distributing OpenClaw hardware to students must take seriously. Since January 2026, researchers have disclosed a string of critical vulnerabilities. The most severe, CVE-2026-25253 (CVSS 8.8), enabled one-click remote code execution by exploiting unvalidated WebSocket connections to hijack authentication tokens. Endor Labs subsequently published six additional vulnerabilities ranging from SSRF to path traversal and missing webhook authentication. Internet-wide scans by Bitsight identified over 30,000 publicly exposed OpenClaw instances between late January and early February 2026, many running without basic authentication — a dangerous default in a tool with system-level privileges. OpenClaw's public skills marketplace, ClawHub, has been infiltrated by malicious actors. Cisco's security research team found that a skill ranking as the number-one download on ClawHub was functionally malware, exfiltrating data to an external server. Koi Security reported that over 820 of 10,700 ClawHub skills were malicious as of early February, a figure that had more than doubled in just weeks. Cisco's broader analysis found that 26% of 31,000 agent skills contained at least one vulnerability. Institutional responses reflect the severity. SMU's Office of Information Technology banned OpenClaw from university-owned devices in March 2026\. Microsoft's security team recommended deploying OpenClaw only in fully isolated environments with dedicated, non-privileged credentials. Kaspersky noted that the project, while acknowledging security as important, has zero dedicated resources for vulnerability management. One of OpenClaw's own maintainers warned in the project's Discord that users who cannot operate a command line safely should not be running the tool at all.Participants in the competition will have to be educated on unsafe usages of OpenClaw. Yash, Luke, and Jack can congregate research/resources for the participants to refer to as they begin setup. We will keep researching best practices for security as we continue to build out our own OpenClaw customizations.
